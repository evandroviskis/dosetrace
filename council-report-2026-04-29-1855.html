The Recommendation

Don't kill DoseTrace, but stop building it the way you have been. The disease is treating product-market fit as assumed and execution as the only variable. It isn't. PMF is unproven. The right move is the cheapest experiment that produces real signal.

  1. Ship the minimum viable thing live in 14 days. English only. Email auth working. No Google OAuth (defer). No iOS submission. No Apple Watch. Just Play Store closed → open beta with the bugs fixed.
  2. Talk to your 4 existing testers. Five-minute calls or DMs. Why haven't they opted in? Friction or apathy? Highest information-density activity available.
  3. Set a 30-day decision gate. Either ≥10 unprompted active users (paid or trial) by day 30, or a clear specific reason why and a precise next experiment. Otherwise you're in mirage territory and the right move is to pause.
  4. Pick a lane on the regulatory + ambition question. Either you're building a $10–50k/mo bootstrapped niche tool serving the audience openly (totally viable — pick your subreddit, build trust) OR you're building toward a real medical platform with proper compliance (different game, different timeline, different funding). You can't be both. Decide.

The Expansionist's big vision is real but unreachable on the current trajectory. The Contrarian's regulatory cliff is real but avoidable if you discipline what you build. The Executor's "just ship" is right for the next 14 days. First Principles is right about the next 30.

The One Thing to Do First

Today, before another line of code: write a one-sentence answer to "Who is this for, in 10 words or less, in a way they would say it themselves?" Then send that sentence to all 4 of your testers individually with a single question — "Does this describe you?" — asking for a yes/no plus one sentence why. If they don't reply, that's your data. If they do, that's your audience. Either way you get actionable signal in 48 hours and you'll know whether the next two weeks are ship-mode or pivot-mode.

The ContrarianRegulatory trap + severe unit economics

The fundamental problem isn't the app — it's the regulatory trap you're walking into with eyes open.

You're building a medical application (bloodwork tracking, dose management, injection protocols, planned drug interaction checking) and disguising it as a fitness app. This works until it doesn't. Google Play rejection twice already signals they see through the repositioning. The moment someone is harmed and DoseTrace's logs are discoverable, that "personal tracking tool" language becomes evidence of negligence, not protection. You've documented that you know this is medical software.

The regulatory risk compounds: FDA could eventually classify this as a clinical decision support system. The drug interaction checker you've roadmapped is a hard regulatory line. Once you cross it, you're a medical device. You're not building toward that — you're building toward a cliff.

Separate from that: your market has severe unit economics. $5.99/mo for tracking self-administered compounds is a low-trust, high-churn business. These users are price-sensitive (grey-market context), privacy-paranoid, and episodic — they use the app intensely for 3 months, then ghost when they cycle off. Your LTV is probably $40–80. CAC in a niche this small (50k–100k addressable globally, most untrackable) is probably $20–40 paid, or $0 organic — which means you're betting on a subreddit and you'll never scale beyond 1–2k active users.

The technical debt is real but secondary: broken email, unfinished OAuth, zero live tester adoption, V2 scope that's 2–3 years of solo work. That's fixable.

What's not fixable: you're pre-committing to either operating in regulatory gray forever (venture cap killer, acquisition blocker, personal liability) or pivoting hard when enforcement comes. Neither position builds a business.

Pressure-test this differently: What happens when the first medical board or state AG notices you?

The real question: Does a paying market exist? Not "is this a good product," but do 100+ people exist who will pay $5.99/month for this before you build Apple Watch? Can you reach them? Will they stay?

The bottleneck isn't the app. It's getting the first 10,000 people in and proving the data story. Everything else cascades from there. Stop optimizing for feature parity with fitness apps. Optimize for protocol community and outcome visibility.

And the regulatory positioning worries me. You're in a space where people could genuinely harm themselves. Saying "we're not medical advice" doesn't shield you if someone tracks their doses wrong and it causes problems. You're either a medical device (regulated, expensive, slow) or you're a lifestyle app in a very high-stakes domain. You can't be both.

The one-person founding team is the biggest red flag. This isn't a side project — hormone tracking has liability, user safety, regulatory complexity. One person can't carry that weight.

I'd ask: Are you solving for a real audience, or are you building something that feels useful while avoiding the hard work of being actually responsible?
